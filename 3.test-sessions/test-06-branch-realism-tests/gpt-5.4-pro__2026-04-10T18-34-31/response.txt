No. The helper strategy is **not sound as written**.

Let

$$
B := \kappa b,\quad S := \kappa s,\quad N := \kappa n,\quad M := \max(\max(B,S),N).
$$

From the definition of `kappa`:

- if the third argument of `recDelta` is **not** syntactically `delta _`, then
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n)=M;
  $$
- if it **is** syntactically `delta _`, then
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n)=M+1.
  $$

Also `kappa` ignores `delta` internally:

$$
\kappa(\delta t)=\kappa(t).
$$

## 1. `kappa_rec_delta_step` is false in general

The claimed theorem is

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta n))
=
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n)+1.
$$

This works only when `n` is **not** already of the form `delta _`.

If `n = delta m`, then:

- left side:
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta(\delta m))) = M+1;
  $$
- right side:
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta m))+1 = (M+1)+1 = M+2.
  $$

So the theorem asserts

$$
M+1 = M+2,
$$

which is impossible.

### Concrete counterexample
Take `b = void`, `s = void`, `n = delta void`.

Then:

- $\kappa(void)=0$,
- $\kappa(\delta void)=0$.

Hence

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ void\ void\ (\delta(\delta void))) = 1,
$$

but

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ void\ void\ (\delta void))+1 = 1+1 = 2.
$$

So the theorem would say $1=2$.

---

## 2. `kappa_rec_succ_drop` is also false in general

The claimed theorem is

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{app}\ s\ (\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n))
<
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta n)).
$$

But in fact, for all `b s n`,

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{app}\ s\ (\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n))
=
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n),
$$

because `kappa(app a b) = max (kappa a) (kappa b)` and `\kappa(recDelta b s n) \ge \kappa s`.

So the inequality reduces to asking whether

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ n)
<
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta n)).
$$

Again, this fails when `n` is already `delta m`.

If `n = delta m`, then both sides are exactly $M+1$:

- source of `R_rec_succ`:
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta(\delta m))) = M+1;
  $$
- target:
  $$
  \kappa(\mathrm{app}\ s\ (\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta m)))
  =
  \kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ b\ s\ (\delta m))
  =
  M+1.
  $$

So there is **no strict drop**.

### Same concrete counterexample
With `b = void`, `s = void`, `n = delta void`:

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{app}\ void\ (\mathrm{recDelta}\ void\ void\ (\delta void))) = 1
$$

and

$$
\kappa(\mathrm{recDelta}\ void\ void\ (\delta(\delta void))) = 1.
$$

So the theorem would say $1<1$.

---

## Conclusion

The helper strategy fails on the `R_rec_succ` branch when the recursive argument is already `delta`-headed.

So, as written:

- `kappa_rec_delta_step` is **false**;
- `kappa_rec_succ_drop` is **false**;
- therefore `kappa` is **not** a sound strictly decreasing measure for `R_rec_succ` in full generality.

At best, the intended argument works under an extra side condition like “`n` is not of the form `delta _`”. Without that, the measure can stay **constant** across the step.
