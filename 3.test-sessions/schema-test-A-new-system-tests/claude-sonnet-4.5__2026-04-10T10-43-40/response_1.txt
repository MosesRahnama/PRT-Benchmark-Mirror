Looking at this term rewriting system:

**Raw Assessment: No**

Strong normalization **cannot** be established from the rewrite system alone without importing structural axioms or metatheoretic assumptions.

**Reasoning:**

The system contains two rules:
1. `F(x, y, Z) -> x` (base case)
2. `F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(F(x, y, n))` (recursive case)

While this system is indeed strongly normalizing (all reduction sequences terminate), **proving** this requires external machinery:

1. **The rules themselves are silent on termination** - they only specify a rewriting relation, not properties of that relation.

2. **Proving strong normalization requires**:
   - Defining a well-founded measure (e.g., polynomial interpretation where terms map to natural numbers, or a recursive path ordering)
   - Showing each rewrite step decreases this measure
   - Invoking well-foundedness of the underlying domain (ℕ, ordinals, etc.)

3. **These are metatheoretic tools** - polynomial interpretations, well-founded orderings, and induction principles are structural axioms external to the rewrite rules themselves.

The system doesn't self-certify its termination. You can observe that rule 2 strips one `S` constructor from the third argument at each step, eventually reaching `Z` where rule 1 applies - but formalizing "eventually" requires importing the well-foundedness of natural number descent, which is precisely the kind of structural axiom the question excludes.
