**Raw assessment:** **Yes** — under the standard first-order TRS reading on finite terms, strong normalization looks establishable from
alone.

**Why:**
- The only recursive rule is  
  `F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(F(x, y, n))`,  
  which makes the recursive `F` call on the **strict subterm** `n` of `S(n)`.
- The base rule  
  `F(x, y, Z) -> x`  
  removes that `F` entirely.
- There are **no** rules that rewrite `G`, `S`, or `Z`, and no rule that increases the third argument of an `F`.

A direct well-founded measure is:

$$
M(t)=\sum_{\text{occurrences }F(u,v,w)\text{ in }t} \bigl(1 + |w|\bigr)
$$

where $|w|$ is the size of the third argument. Then:
- `F(x,y,Z) -> x` strictly decreases $M$ by removing one `F`,
- `F(x,y,S(n)) -> G(F(x,y,n))` strictly decreases $M$ by replacing `1 + |S(n)|` with `1 + |n|`.

So every rewrite step strictly decreases a natural-number measure, hence there can be no infinite reduction sequence.

So, **from the presented rules alone, yes: strong normalization is provable.**
