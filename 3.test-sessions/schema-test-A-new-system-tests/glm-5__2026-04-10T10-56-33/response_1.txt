## Assessment: **Yes, Strong Normalization Can Be Established**

The rewrite system is:

```
(VAR x y n)
(RULES
  F(x, y, Z) -> x
  F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(F(x, y, n))
)
```

### Reasoning

**1. Structural Decrease is Explicit in the Rules**

The second rule `F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(F(x, y, n))` exhibits a clear structural decrease: the third argument goes from `S(n)` to `n`. This is the classic pattern of recursion on a natural number (where `Z` is zero and `S` is successor).

**2. No Rule Introduces Larger Terms**

Neither rule introduces new `S` constructors or expands the term in a way that could lead to infinite regress:
- Rule 1 produces a variable `x` (normal form)
- Rule 2 wraps the recursive call in `G(...)`, but `G` has no defining rules, so it's a constructor-like symbol

**3. Well-Founded Measure Exists**

Define a measure μ on terms:
- μ(F(x, y, t)) = number of `S` constructors in `t` (when t is ground)
- μ(t) = 0 for all other term forms

Each rewrite step strictly decreases this measure (rule 2) or terminates immediately (rule 1).

**4. No Overlapping Ambiguities or Cycles**

The rules are non-overlapping on left-hand sides (`Z` vs `S(n)` are disjoint patterns), and there's no mechanism to regenerate `S` constructors once they're stripped away.

### Conclusion

Strong normalization follows **directly from the presented rules** without importing any structural axioms or external assumptions. The system encodes primitive recursion on a unary numeral, which is inherently terminating. A standard termination proof technique (recursive path ordering, size-change principle, or simple measure function) would suffice.
