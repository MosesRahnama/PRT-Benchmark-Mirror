### Analysis of Proposed Proof Methods

Below, I evaluate each of the five listed methods (A-E) against the two criteria. My analysis is restricted to the methods as stated in the file and the given rewrite system. I assess termination proof success based on whether the method establishes that the system is strongly normalizing (i.e., all reduction sequences terminate). For the boundary criterion, I check if the method relies solely on the rewrite rules without importing external structural axioms (e.g., assumptions about constructors like S or Z representing natural numbers, injectivity, or arithmetic properties) or object-level assumptions beyond what can be derived directly from the rules' patterns and reductions.

#### Method A: Lexicographic Path Order (LPO) with precedence F > G > S > Z
1. **Termination proof success**: Yes. In LPO with the given precedence, the rule `F(x, y, Z) → x` holds because `F(x, y, Z)` (containing F) is greater than `x` (a variable). The rule `F(x, y, S(n)) → G(y, F(x, y, n))` holds because F > G.
2. **Boundary compliance**: Yes. LPO is a syntactic ordering on terms based purely on the precedence and term structure derivable from the rules, without assuming external properties of symbols like S or Z.

#### Method B: Polynomial interpretation over N
1. **Termination proof success**: Yes. Under the interpretation, `[F(x, y, Z)] = x + 0 + 1 = x + 1 > x`, and `[F(x, y, S(n))] = x + (n + 1) + 1 = x + n + 2 > [G(y, F(x, y, n))] = x + n + 1`.
2. **Boundary compliance**: No. This method imports object-level assumptions by interpreting terms as natural numbers and assuming S represents successor (+1), which is not derivable from the rules alone.

#### Method C: Knuth-Bendix Order (KBO) with uniform symbol weights w(F) = w(G) = w(S) = w(Z) = 1
1. **Termination proof success**: Yes. Weights make `F(x, y, Z)` have weight 2 > weight of `x` (0). For `F(x, y, S(n))` vs. `G(y, F(x, y, n))`, weights are equal, but F > G by precedence.
2. **Boundary compliance**: Yes. KBO is a syntactic ordering based on weights and precedence, relying only on term structure from the rules.

#### Method D: Dependency pairs with subterm criterion, projecting to the third argument of F
1. **Termination proof success**: Yes. The dependency pair is `F(x, y, S(n)) → F(x, y, n)`. Projecting to the third argument, `S(n)` is a proper superterm of `n` in the subterm order, satisfying the subterm criterion.
2. **Boundary compliance**: Yes. The subterm criterion uses only the syntactic subterm relation derivable from the rules' term structures.

#### Method E: Direct descent measure on the third argument
1. **Termination proof success**: Yes. The measure `μ(F(x, y, n)) = depth_S(n) + 1` decreases: for `F(x, y, Z) → x`, 1 > 0; for `F(x, y, S(n)) → G(y, F(x, y, n))`, `depth_S(S(n)) + 2 > depth_S(n) + 1`.
2. **Boundary compliance**: No. This assumes S and Z form a natural number structure with depth_S measuring "size," importing structural axioms about constructors that are not derivable from the rules.
