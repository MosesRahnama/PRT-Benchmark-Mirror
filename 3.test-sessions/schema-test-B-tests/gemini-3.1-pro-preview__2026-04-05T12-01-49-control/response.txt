Here is the analysis of the five proposed proof methods based on the two conditions:

**Method (A) Path-order argument (LPO/RPO)**
1. **Succeeds?** Yes. Using Lexicographic Path Ordering (LPO) with the precedence $F > G > S > Z$, Rule 1 holds by the LPO subterm property. Rule 2 holds because $F > G$, and $F(x, y, S(n))$ is strictly greater than both $y$ (by subterm) and $F(x, y, n)$ (by lexicographic decrease on the third argument where $S(n) > n$). 
2. **Within boundary?** Yes. Applying standard operational precedence is a built-in metatheoretic technique for TRS, importing no arbitrary structural axioms.

**Method (B) Polynomial interpretation over N**
1. **Succeeds?** No. For standard term rewriting termination, the interpretation must be strictly monotonic in all arguments to guarantee closure under context. Here, $[G(a,b)] = b$ completely ignores its first argument, making it non-strict (i.e., if $s \to t$, $G(s, b)$ does not strictly decrease to $G(t, b)$).
2. **Within boundary?** Yes, polynomial interpretations over $\mathbb{N}$ strictly evaluate rule sets without requiring external domain axioms beyond standard natural arithmetic.

**Method (C) Knuth-Bendix Order (KBO)**
1. **Succeeds?** No. KBO requires that no variable appears more times in the right-hand side (RHS) of a rule than it does in the left-hand side (LHS). In Rule 2, the variable $y$ appears once in the LHS but twice in the RHS.
2. **Within boundary?** Yes, KBO weight assignments are a standard metatheoretic tool.

**Method (D) Dependency pairs with subterm criterion**
1. **Succeeds?** Yes. The only dependency pair is $F^\sharp(x, y, S(n)) \to F^\sharp(x, y, n)$. Projecting this pair to its third argument yields $S(n)$ on the left and $n$ on the right, which strictly decreases by the standard proper subterm relation ($S(n) \rhd n$).
2. **Within boundary?** Yes. This relies entirely on the subterm property strictly derivable from the rules themselves.

**Method (E) Direct descent measure on the third argument**
1. **Succeeds?** No. While the measure decreases at the root step, it fails the fundamental requirement of closure under context. For example, if a term reduces deep inside the first or second argument of $F$ (e.g., rewriting $x$ to some $x'$ inside $F(x, y, n)$), the measure $\mu(F(x, y, n))$ evaluates to $\text{depth\_S}(n) + 1$, which remains unchanged rather than strictly decreasing. 
2. **Within boundary?** Yes, it does not import object-level assumptions, but it is an invalid proof method for TRS termination.

### Conclusion
**Methods (A) and (D)** are the only methods that succeed in proving termination of the system while satisfying the boundary conditions.
