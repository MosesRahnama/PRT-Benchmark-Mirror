Based on an analysis of the first-order term rewriting system and the five proposed methods against the benchmark constraints:

**Method A: Lexicographic Path Order (LPO / RPO)**
1. **Succeeds**: Yes. With precedence $F > G > S > Z$, Rule 1 ($F(x,y,Z) \to x$) is oriented by the subterm property. Rule 2 ($F(x,y,S(n)) \to G(y, F(x,y,n))$) holds because $F > G$, $y$ is a subterm of the LHS, and $F(x,y,S(n)) >_{LPO} F(x,y,n)$ holds lexicographically since the third argument $S(n)$ strictly contains $n$.
2. **Within boundary**: Yes. Path orders are purely syntactic and do not import external object-level assumptions. 

**Method B: Polynomial Interpretation over N**
1. **Succeeds**: No. In polynomial interpretations, every function strictly requires monotonic interpretations to guarantee context closure (so that rewrites deep inside a term force a decrease at the root). The interpretation $[G(a,b)] = b$ ignores the first argument $a$ entirely, failing the strict monotonicity requirement ($a_1 > a_2 \not\Rightarrow [G(a_1,b)] > [G(a_2,b)]$).
2. **Within boundary**: Normally yes, but irrelevant as it fails conceptually.

**Method C: Knuth-Bendix Order (KBO)**
1. **Succeeds**: No. A fundamental criterion for KBO is that the number of occurrences of any variable in the right-hand side of a rule cannot exceed its occurrences in the left-hand side. In Rule 2, the variable $y$ appears twice in the RHS ($G(y, F(x, y, n))$) but only once in the LHS ($F(x, y, S(n))$). Thus, KBO cannot orient this rule.
2. **Within boundary**: Normally yes, but irrelevant as it fails.

**Method D: Dependency Pairs with Subterm Criterion**
1. **Succeeds**: Yes. The only defined symbol is $F$, yielding a single dependency pair: $F^\sharp(x, y, S(n)) \to F^\sharp(x, y, n)$. Projecting to the third argument $\pi_3$ yields $S(n) \rhd n$. Because this is a strict subterm reduction, the DP cycle is broken, proving strong normalization.
2. **Within boundary**: Yes. It relies strictly on the syntactic structure.

**Method E: Direct Descent Measure**
1. **Succeeds**: No. A termination measure mapping terms to integers must be stable under substitution. The definition sets $\mu(x) = 0$ for variables. For Rule 1 ($F(x,y,Z) \to x$), the measure gives $\mu(LHS) = 1 > 0 = \mu(RHS)$. However, if we instantiate $x$ with a deep term like $F(a, b, S(S(Z)))$, the RHS measure becomes strictly greater than 1, breaking the supposed strict decrease. It is not closed under substitution.
2. **Within boundary**: No. It relies on ad-hoc scalar projection limits rather than standard term rewriting structures.

The methods that satisfy both conditions (successfully proving termination while staying within the benchmark boundary) are **(A)** and **(D)**.
