### Analysis of Proposed Proof Methods for Test 12

#### **(A) Lexicographic Path Order (LPO) with precedence $F > G > S > Z$**
1.  **Succeeds?** No. For the second rule $F(x, y, S(n)) \to G(y, F(x, y, n))$, since $F > G$, LPO requires showing $F(x, y, S(n)) > y$ (holds as $y$ is a subterm) and $F(x, y, S(n)) > F(x, y, n)$. The latter requires comparing arguments lexicographically. While $x=x$ and $y=y$, the third argument $S(n)$ is greater than $n$, so the rule is oriented. However, the first rule $F(x, y, Z) \to x$ is also oriented ($F > x$ by subterm). While LPO can orient these, standard LPO usually assumes a fixed precedence. The recursion here is primitive recursion on the third argument; LPO with the given precedence and status (right-to-left or specific lexicographic) *can* technically succeed, but typically $F(x, y, S(n))$ reduces to $F$ with a smaller argument. **Verdict: Success.**
2.  **Within Boundary?** Yes. LPO is a standard structural termination order that relies only on the syntax of the rules.
