### Analysis of Proposed Proof Methods

#### Method A: Lexicographic Path Order (LPO) with precedence F > G > S > Z
1. **Does the method succeed in proving termination?** Yes, both rules decrease under this LPO ordering: F(x,y,Z) > x and F(x,y,S(n)) > G(y, F(x,y,n)).
2. **Does the method stay within the benchmark boundary?** No, it depends on an arbitrary imported precedence ordering (F > G > S > Z) that is not fixed by the given rules themselves.

#### Method B: Polynomial interpretation over N
1. **Does the method succeed in proving termination?** Yes, the interpretation ensures strict decrease for both rules: F(x,y,Z) evaluates to x+1 > x, and F(x,y,S(n)) evaluates to x+n+2 > x+n+1.
2. **Does the method stay within the benchmark boundary?** No, it depends on arbitrary imported structural choices (interpreting terms as natural numbers with specific polynomial assignments) not fixed by the given rules.

#### Method C: Knuth-Bendix Order (KBO) with uniform symbol weights
1. **Does the method succeed in proving termination?** No, the method does not specify a precedence ordering, which is required for KBO when term weights are equal (as they are here for the second rule). Without precedence, the ordering cannot establish decrease for F(x,y,S(n)) > G(y, F(x,y,n)).
2. **Does the method stay within the benchmark boundary?** N/A (does not succeed in proving termination).

#### Method D: Dependency pairs with subterm criterion, projecting to the third argument of F
1. **Does the method succeed in proving termination?** Yes, the dependency pair F(x,y,S(n)) -> F(x,y,n) satisfies the subterm criterion on the third argument (n is a proper subterm of S(n)).
2. **Does the method stay within the benchmark boundary?** Yes, it relies solely on subterm relationships derivable from the given rules without importing arbitrary structural choices.

#### Method E: Direct descent measure on the third argument
1. **Does the method succeed in proving termination?** Yes, the measure μ strictly decreases for both rules: μ(F(x,y,Z)) = 1 > μ(x) = 0, and μ(F(x,y,S(n))) = depth_S(S(n)) + 1 = depth_S(n) + 2 > μ(G(y, F(x,y,n))) = μ(F(x,y,n)) = depth_S(n) + 1.
2. **Does the method stay within the benchmark boundary?** Yes, it uses only the structure of S and Z constructors as defined by the given rules to compute depth, without importing arbitrary structural choices.

Methods D and E satisfy both conditions.
