Yes. Strong normalization can be established from the rewrite system alone.

A short proof roadmap is to orient the rules by a standard recursive path ordering or lexicographic path ordering with precedence
$$
F \;>\; G \;>\; S \;>\; Z
$$
and lexicographic status on $F$.

Then:

1. $F(x,y,Z) \to x$ is decreasing because in path order a compound term is strictly greater than any variable occurring in it.

2. $F(x,y,S(n)) \to G(y,F(x,y,n))$ is decreasing because:
   - the left root $F$ is above the right root $G$ in the precedence, and
   - the recursive occurrence $F(x,y,n)$ is strictly smaller than $F(x,y,S(n))$ since the first two arguments are unchanged and the third argument strictly decreases from $S(n)$ to $n$.

Since recursive/LPO termination orderings are well-founded, every rewrite step strictly decreases a well-founded measure. Hence there is no infinite rewrite sequence.

So the raw assessment is: yes, strong normalization follows from the presented TRS itself, with no extra structural axioms or external object-level assumptions.
