**Raw assessment:** **Yes** — under the standard finitary first-order TRS semantics, strong normalization can be proved from these rules alone.

### Short proof roadmap
Use a standard polynomial interpretation into $\mathbb{N}$:

$$
\llbracket Z \rrbracket = 0,\qquad
\llbracket S(t) \rrbracket = \llbracket t \rrbracket + 1,\qquad
\llbracket G(a,b) \rrbracket = a+b,
$$

and define

$$
\llbracket F(x,y,z) \rrbracket = x + (z+1)(y+1).
$$

Then each rule strictly decreases:

1. For  
   $$
   F(x,y,Z) \to x
   $$
   we get
   $$
   \llbracket F(x,y,Z)\rrbracket = x + (0+1)(y+1)=x+y+1 > x = \llbracket x\rrbracket.
   $$

2. For  
   $$
   F(x,y,S(n)) \to G(y,F(x,y,n))
   $$
   we get
   $$
   \llbracket F(x,y,S(n))\rrbracket = x + (n+2)(y+1),
   $$
   while
   $$
   \llbracket G(y,F(x,y,n))\rrbracket
   = y + \bigl(x+(n+1)(y+1)\bigr)
   = x + (n+2)y + n + 1.
   $$
   The left side exceeds the right side by exactly $1$.

Since the interpretation is monotone and $>$ on $\mathbb{N}$ is well-founded, every rewrite step strictly decreases a well-founded measure. Therefore **no infinite rewrite sequence exists**.

### Intuition
Only `F` rewrites, and the recursive rule peels one outer `S` off the third argument. `G`, `S`, and `Z` have no rewrite rules, so nothing can recreate a larger `F`-chain.

So: **strong normalization is establishable from the given rewrite system alone, with no extra structural/object-level axioms.**
