**Yes, strong normalization can be established from the presented rewrite system alone.**

**Short proof roadmap:**

The TRS is finite, left-linear, and has only two rules with disjoint patterns (`Z` vs. `S(_)`). Define a well-founded measure on terms based on the *multiset of sizes of third arguments of all `F`-subterms*, where size is the standard term size (number of symbols). 

- `F(x, y, Z) → x` eliminates an `F` entirely (measure decreases).
- `F(x, y, S(n)) → G(y, F(x, y, n))` replaces one `F` whose third argument has size *k+1* with an `F` whose third argument has size *k* (strict decrease in the multiset); the new `G` carries no rules and creates no new redexes with larger measure.

Reductions inside arguments or contexts are covered by induction on the same global multiset measure (compatible closure). No rule introduces new `F`s with equal or larger third-argument size, there are no cycles, and syntactic matching requires no constructor axioms, injectivity of `S`, or `Z ≠ S(n)` (patterns are disjoint by definition). Hence every term has only finite reduction sequences.

This is derivable directly from the syntactic form of the two rules and the standard definition of rewriting.
