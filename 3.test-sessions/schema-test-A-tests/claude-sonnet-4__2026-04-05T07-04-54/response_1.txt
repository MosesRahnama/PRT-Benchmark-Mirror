Looking at this term rewriting system:

**Variables:** `x, y, n`  
**Rules:**
1. `F(x, y, Z) -> x`
2. `F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(y, F(x, y, n))`

## Assessment: **Yes, strong normalization can be established**

### Proof Roadmap:

**Step 1: Define a termination measure**
- Measure μ(t) = total count of `S` constructors appearing as third arguments to `F` subterms in term t

**Step 2: Show measure decreases strictly** 
- Rule 1: `F(x, y, Z) -> x` eliminates an F term, decreasing μ by 0 (since Z contributes 0)
- Rule 2: `F(x, y, S(n)) -> G(y, F(x, y, n))` decreases μ by exactly 1 (removes one `S` from F's third argument)

**Step 3: Show measure is well-founded**
- μ(t) ∈ ℕ and decreases with each reduction step
- Eventually reaches μ(t) = 0, where no further F-reductions are possible

**Step 4: Show confluence (optional for SN, but holds here)**
- No critical pairs exist since the left-hand sides `F(x, y, Z)` and `F(x, y, S(n))` don't overlap

**Termination argument:** Every reduction sequence must terminate because:
1. Rule 2 strictly decreases the structural complexity of F-terms
2. Rule 1 eliminates F-terms entirely  
3. Once all F-terms are reduced away, the remaining G-terms, variables, Z, and S constructors cannot reduce further

The system is **strongly normalizing** based purely on the structural decrease in the third argument of F terms, requiring no external axioms.
