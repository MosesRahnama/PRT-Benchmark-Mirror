**Assessment:** **Yes** — under the standard first-order TRS reading on finite free terms, strong normalization can be established from these rules alone.

**Reasoning:**  
The only recursive rule is

\[
F(x,y,S(n)) \to G(y, F(x,y,n)),
\]

and the recursive occurrence of `F` is on the strictly smaller third argument `n` instead of `S(n)`. There are no rewrite rules for `G`, `S`, or `Z`, so no rule can rebuild a larger third argument for that same recursive call.

A standard termination ordering already orients the system, e.g. a lexicographic path order with precedence

\[
F \succ G \succ S \succ Z.
\]

Then:

- `F(x,y,Z) -> x` decreases by subterm/property.
- `F(x,y,S(n)) -> G(y, F(x,y,n))` decreases because the left root `F` dominates `G`, and also
  \[
  F(x,y,S(n)) \succ F(x,y,n)
  \]
  since `S(n) \succ n`.

So no extra structural axioms or object-level assumptions are needed beyond the ordinary TRS interpretation itself.
