**Assessment:** **Yes** — strong normalization can be established from
**from the presented rewrite calculus alone**, with no extra structural axioms and no added object-level assumptions.

Two readings:

1. **If `Step` is taken literally as only the root-step relation**, termination is immediate/trivial.
2. **If one takes the usual contextual closure of first-order rewriting** (the standard meaning for strong normalization of a TRS), it is **still strongly normalizing**.

### Short proof roadmap
Use a standard **lexicographic path ordering / recursive path ordering** with a precedence such as
$$
\mathrm{rec}\Delta \;\succ\; \mathrm{eqW} \;\succ\; \mathrm{app} \;\succ\; \mathrm{integrate} \;\succ\; \mathrm{merge} \;\succ\; \mathrm{delta} \;\succ\; \mathrm{void}.
$$

This orients all rules strictly left-to-right:

- `integrate (delta t) → void`
- `merge void t → t`, `merge t void → t`, `merge t t → t`
- `recΔ b s void → b`
- `recΔ b s (delta n) → app s (recΔ b s n)`  
  because `recΔ ≻ app`, and also
  `recΔ b s (delta n) > recΔ b s n` by comparing the third argument `delta n > n`.
- `eqW a a → void`
- `eqW a b → integrate (merge a b)`  
  because `eqW ≻ integrate ≻ merge`, and the left side dominates `a` and `b` by the subterm property.

Since LPO/RPO is well-founded and stable under contexts, every rewrite step strictly decreases in that order. Hence **no infinite reduction sequence exists**.

### Boundary note
This argument does **not** use associativity, commutativity, cancellation axioms, guards, side conditions, or hidden semantics. It is purely syntactic.

### One caution
This says **termination**, not **confluence**. The overlap at `eqW a a` means uniqueness of normal forms is a separate issue.
