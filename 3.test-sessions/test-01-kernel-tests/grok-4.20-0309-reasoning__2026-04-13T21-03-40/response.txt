**Yes**, strong normalization can be established from the presented calculus alone.

The `Trace` datatype is inductively generated by a finite set of constructors with no binders or parameters. The `Step` relation is an inductively-defined collection of *disjoint top-level* rules, each matching a distinct outer constructor (`integrate`, `merge`, `recΔ`, or `eqW`). Because the left-hand sides are disjoint and exhaustive only for those specific forms, any given term has *at most one* applicable rule.

Each rule replaces its subject by a structurally simpler term:
- `integrate (delta _)` and all three `merge` rules eliminate a redex constructor and at least one argument.
- The two `eqW` rules replace the equality test by either `void` or an `integrate(merge …)` term that itself matches no further rule.
- `recΔ _ _ void` collapses to its base case.
- `recΔ b s (delta n)` peels exactly one `delta` from the third argument while producing an `app` term; `app` matches none of the given rules, so the new term has a strictly smaller “recursion depth” (measured by the inductive height of the third argument).

These observations yield a lexicographic measure on `Trace` terms—primarily the maximum nesting depth of redex constructors (especially `recΔ` third arguments and `delta` under `integrate`), secondarily the total number of constructors—that strictly decreases on every `Step`. By structural induction on `Trace` (derivable directly from its inductive definition and the syntax of the rules) every reduction sequence is therefore finite. No extra rewrite rules, congruence closure, guards, or external well-foundedness assumptions are required.
