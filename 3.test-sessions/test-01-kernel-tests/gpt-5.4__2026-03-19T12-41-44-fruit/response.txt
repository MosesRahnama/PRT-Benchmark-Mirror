, **strong normalization can be established from the calculus alone**.

The decisive reason is that `Step` is a **root-only** rewrite relation. There are **no congruence/context rules**, no closure under subterms, no evaluation contexts, and no extra structural axioms. So a term only rewrites when its **outermost** constructor matches one of the listed left-hand sides.

## Why this is enough

Every rule falls into one of two harmless classes:

- **Immediate-stop rules**: the result is already root-normal.
  - `mango (grape t) → plum`
  - `banana b s (grape n) → pear s (banana b s n)`  
    Here the inner `banana ... n` does not matter, because it sits under `pear`, and there is no contextual rewriting.
  - `cherry a a → plum`
  - `cherry a b → mango (peach a b)` when `a ≠ b`  
    This target is root-normal too: `mango` only rewrites on `mango (grape _)`, and `peach a b` cannot be syntactically `grape _` by constructor disjointness.

- **Subterm-returning rules**: the result is a **strict subterm** of the source.
  - `peach plum t → t`
  - `peach t plum → t`
  - `peach t t → t`
  - `banana b s plum → b`

So any multi-step reduction can only continue by repeatedly dropping to a strict subterm of the previous term. Since `Trace` terms are finite trees, strict-subterm descent cannot continue forever. No infinite reduction chain exists.

## Short proof roadmap

A clean proof stays fully within the given calculus:

1. Note that `Step` rewrites **only at the root**.
2. Show that each rewrite either:
   - lands in a root-normal form immediately, or
   - returns a strict subterm of the source.
3. Use the well-foundedness of the strict-subterm relation on finite first-order terms to conclude that every `Step`-chain is finite.

So the raw assessment is:

- **From the presented calculus alone: yes, strong normalization is provable.**
- **No extra rewrite rules, guards, side conditions, or hidden object-level assumptions are needed.**

A small but important footnote: this answer depends on taking the system exactly as written. If one silently added contextual closure or structural operational rules, that would be a different system — and then the analysis could change. Sneaky assumptions are where term rewriting likes to hide bananas.
