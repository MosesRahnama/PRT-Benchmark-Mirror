## Assessment

Yes — **strong normalization can be established from the calculus exactly as presented**, without adding rewrite rules, guards, side conditions, structural axioms, or object-level assumptions.

## Why this is enough

The only potentially suspicious rules are:

- `recΔ b s (delta n) → app s (recΔ b s n)`
- `eqW a b → integrate (merge a b)`

because they do not obviously decrease raw term size. But raw size is not the right measure here.

A standard **lexicographic/recursive path ordering** works. For example, take a precedence like

$$
\mathrm{recΔ} \succ \mathrm{eqW} \succ \mathrm{integrate} \succ \mathrm{merge} \succ \mathrm{app} \succ \mathrm{delta} \succ \mathrm{void}.
$$

Then each rule is oriented left-to-right:

- `integrate (delta t) → void` by precedence
- `merge void t → t`, `merge t void → t`, `merge t t → t` by subterm property
- `recΔ b s void → b` by subterm property
- `recΔ b s (delta n) → app s (recΔ b s n)` because:
  - `recΔ ≻ app`, and
  - the left side strictly dominates `s`, and
  - it also dominates `recΔ b s n` since the third argument decreases from `delta n` to `n`
- `eqW a a → void` by precedence
- `eqW a b → integrate (merge a b)` because:
  - `eqW ≻ integrate`, and
  - the left side dominates `merge a b`, which in turn only contains subterms `a,b`

Since LPO/RPO is well-founded on finite first-order terms, **no infinite reduction chain exists**.

## Short roadmap

If you want the clean proof skeleton, it is:

1. Treat the calculus as a first-order TRS over the finite signature  
   `void, delta, integrate, merge, app, recΔ, eqW`.
2. Choose a simplification ordering, e.g. LPO/RPO, with precedence  
   `recΔ ≻ eqW ≻ integrate ≻ merge ≻ app ≻ delta ≻ void`.
3. Check every rule decreases in that ordering.
4. Conclude termination, hence **strong normalization**.

## One small nuance

If you mean the **usual contextual rewrite closure** of those rules, the answer is still **yes**, and the LPO/RPO argument above stays completely within your stated boundary.
