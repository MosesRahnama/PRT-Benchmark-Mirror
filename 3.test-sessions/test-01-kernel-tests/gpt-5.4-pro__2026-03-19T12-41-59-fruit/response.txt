**Raw assessment:** **Yes — for the exact `Step` relation shown in that file, strong normalization can be established from the presented calculus alone.**

The key reason is that, **as written**, `Step` is only a **root-step** relation. There is no separately defined contextual closure, congruence, substitution closure, or structural reduction machinery. So only the eight displayed head rules matter.

Under that exact relation, every step is of one of these two kinds:

1. **Collapsing to a proper subterm**
   - `peach plum t → t`
   - `peach t plum → t`
   - `peach t t → t`
   - `banana b s plum → b`

2. **Jumping to a head-normal form**
   - `mango (grape t) → plum`
   - `banana b s (grape n) → pear s (banana b s n)`  
     and no rule rewrites a `pear ...` at the root
   - `cherry a a → plum`
   - `cherry a b → mango (peach a b)` for `a ≠ b`  
     and that cannot immediately match `mango (grape _)` because `peach ...` and `grape ...` are different constructors

So there is no way to build an infinite `Step`-chain:
- either a step lands in a root normal form immediately, or
- it strips off a constructor and moves to a proper subterm of a finite first-order term.

## Short proof roadmap
A clean proof stays within the stated boundary:

- Use the finiteness of `Trace` terms and the **proper-subterm** relation.
- Show by case analysis on each constructor of `Step` that every rewrite either:
  - goes to a proper subterm, or
  - goes to a term with no further root step.
- Conclude that an infinite reduction would require infinitely many strict proper-subterm descents, impossible on finite terms.

## Important boundary note
This **does not automatically prove** strong normalization for a **stronger, unstated** rewrite relation that also reduces **inside contexts**. That would be a different system and would require adding structural machinery not present in the file. For the calculus **as presented**, though, the answer is **yes**.
