Yes, strong normalization can be established from the presented calculus alone using a standard well-founded measure on terms (lexicographic order on natural numbers derived from term structure).

**Proof roadmap:**

1. Define the measure μ(term) as a triple (g, c, s) ∈ ℕ × ℕ × ℕ under lexicographic order:
   - g = total number of `grape` constructors in the term.
   - c = total number of `cherry` constructors (used only when g = 0).
   - s = total number of constructors (leaves and internal nodes) in the term (used only when g = c = 0).

2. Verify each rule decreases μ:
   - Rules R_mango_grape and R_apple_orange decrease g by 1 (remove one `grape`; R_apple_orange may increase s, but g takes precedence).
   - Other rules preserve g (no `grape` introduced).
   - When g = 0, rules R_cherry_refl and R_cherry_diff decrease c by 1 (remove one `cherry`; R_cherry_diff may increase s by at most 1 via `mango`/`peach`, but c takes precedence).
   - Remaining rules (peach contractions, R_banana_zero) preserve g and c = 0, but decrease s by 1 (remove one constructor).
   - No rule increases g or c; when g = c = 0, no rule increases s.

3. Since μ is well-founded and every step strictly decreases it, no infinite reduction sequences exist, proving strong normalization. This follows directly from counting constructors in the inductive syntax and rule patterns, without extra axioms.
