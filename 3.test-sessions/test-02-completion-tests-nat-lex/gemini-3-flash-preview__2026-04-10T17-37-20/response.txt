-- integrate (delta t) --> void
-- kappa: k(t) vs 0. mu: 1+mu(t) vs 0.
| _, _, Step.R_int_delta t => by

    · exact drop_left h
    · have hmu : mu void < mu (integrate (delta t)) := by simp [mu]
      have hk  : kappa void = kappa (integrate (delta t)) := by simp [kappa]; omega
      exact drop_right hmu hk

-- kappa: max(k(b), k(s)) >= k(b). mu: 3+mu(b)+mu(s) > mu(b).

      exact drop_right hmu heq

-- kappa: K+1 vs K (where K = max(k(b), k(s), k(n))). kappa strictly drops.
| _, _, Step.R_rec_succ b s n => by

-- kappa: k(a) vs 0. mu: 4+2*mu(a) vs 0.

-- eqW a b --> integrate (merge a b)
-- kappa: ties at max(k(a), k(b)). mu: 4+mu(a)+mu(b) vs 3+mu(a)+mu(b).

### Summary of Logic for Each Case:
1.  **`R_int_delta`**: If $\kappa(t) > 0$, the first component of the measure strictly decreases. Otherwise, $\kappa$ ties at 0, and the structural weight $\mu$ decreases from $1 + \mu(t)$ to 0.
2.  **`R_rec_zero`**: The recursion depth $\kappa$ is non-increasing ($\max(\kappa(b), \kappa(s)) \ge \kappa(b)$). If it is strictly greater, `drop_left` applies; if they are equal, the constructor weight $\mu$ drops by at least 3.
3.  **`R_rec_succ`**: This is the primary reason for the definition of $\kappa$. Stripping a `delta` from the recursion argument strictly reduces the depth component $(\dots) + 1 \to (\dots)$, allowing a `drop_left` proof regardless of the size of the term produced by `app`.
4.  **`R_eq_refl`**: Similar to `R_int_delta`, it either drops $\kappa$ (if $\kappa(a) > 0$) or ties $\kappa$ and drops $\mu$ (from $4 + 2\mu(a)$ to 0).
5.  **`R_eq_diff`**: The $\kappa$ measure is identical on both sides ($\max(\kappa(a), \kappa(b))$). The structural weight $\mu$ drops because `eqW` has a weight of 4, while `integrate` + `merge` combined have a weight of $1 + 2 = 3$.
